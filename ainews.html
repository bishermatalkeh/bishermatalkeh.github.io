Deloitte: AI Trends and Early Predictions for 2026

Week 1 | January 2026 | Agentic AI Physical AI Sovereign AI
Prompt: Please summarize this article. The summary should highlight: 1) Main findings and arguments, 2) Broader implications for society, 3) Relevance or potential influence on the architecture, engineering, and/or construction industry.

Main Findings and Arguments:

  • Deloitte highlights three forces shaping near-term AI: agentic AI, physical AI, and sovereign AI.
  • Agentic AI = autonomous systems that can adapt, make decisions, and collaborate with humans/other agents — aimed at automating dynamic, multi-step processes, not just repetitive tasks.
  • Deloitte's survey takeaway: agentic AI adoption is early (many orgs are piloting or haven't deployed), but public sentiment is optimistic about near-term organizational impact.
  • Physical AI combines AI with robotics/IoT/autonomous systems/digital twins to sense-interpret-act in the physical world; adoption depends on barriers like safety/security requirements, costs, integration, workforce readiness, and public acceptance.
  • Sovereign AI focuses on keeping data, models, and compute within national/regional boundaries to address privacy, regulatory, and geopolitical concerns.
  • Deloitte's early predictions for 2026 include: scaling agentic pilots to production plus stronger governance and "agent ops"; paced physical-AI adoption with emphasis on safety/security and human-machine teaming; and increased regulatory scrutiny driving demand for sovereign solutions and regional AI hubs.

Broader Implications for Society:

  • Work changes from "doing tasks" to "managing systems." Agentic AI could shift roles toward supervising/steering automated workflows, increasing the value of judgment, oversight, and governance.
  • Safety and accountability become more visible as AI moves into the physical world (robots, autonomous systems), raising expectations for safeguards, cybersecurity, and auditability.
  • Data sovereignty and regulation will shape who can use what AI where. Sovereign AI implies a future with more localization, compliance, and potentially fragmented AI ecosystems by region.
  • Trust and governance become "board-level" concerns as privacy disputes, cyberattacks, and new regulations pressure organizations to formalize AI controls.
AEC Relevance:
  • Agentic AI in project delivery: Could run multi-step workflows like drafting RFIs/submittals, summarizing specs, tracking action items, or monitoring compliance checklists — then escalating exceptions to humans.
  • Physical AI on jobsites: Deloitte's "physical AI" concept maps directly to AEC tools like robotics, autonomous equipment, sensors/IoT, and digital twins for safety monitoring, progress tracking, and equipment operations.
  • Sovereign AI for owners and public projects: AEC often handles sensitive facility and infrastructure data; sovereign AI concepts are relevant when clients require strict data residency, especially for public sector or regulated facilities.

Behind the Numbers Podcast: How AGI Will Change Our Lives

Week 2 | February 2026 | AGI Workforce Ethics Governance
Source: EMARKETER "Behind the Numbers" Podcast — Episode featuring analysts Jacob Bourne and Grace Harmon discussing AGI's impact on work, society, and when it might arrive.
Prompt: Please summarize this script. The summary should highlight: 1) Main findings and arguments, 2) Broader implications for society, 3) Relevance or potential influence on the architecture, engineering, and/or construction industry.

Main Findings and Arguments:

  • AGI's biggest near-term impact is the workplace. If AI reaches human-level capability, it could reshape what "work" means — either by shifting people into more relationship-heavy roles while AI handles "grunt work," or by pushing society toward universal basic income and large-scale job displacement.
  • The key variable is autonomy + trust. The tipping point is: How much can organizations rely on AI without human supervision? If AI still needs oversight, humans become "AI supervisors."
  • People's preferences and trust matter as much as capability. Even if AI performs well, many people prefer interacting with humans — and may distrust or devalue AI-created content once they know it's AI.
  • AGI raises a governance/consent problem. Using AI isn't the same as informed consent — people may use it out of fear of falling behind at work.
  • Safety and regulation are hard because AGI doesn't exist yet. Compared to nuclear/bioweapons treaties, AI is harder to regulate preemptively because exact capabilities and harms are uncertain.
  • Predictions cluster around "by ~2030," but there's no consensus. Estimates range from next year to centuries away, partly because definitions differ.

Broader Implications for Society:

  • Economic power concentrates further in big tech. Wealth and influence accumulating among major AI firms gives them outsized political and legal sway.
  • Major shifts in human identity and social fabric. AI expanding into companionship, coaching, and mental-health support raises questions about dependence, authenticity, and human connection.
  • High-stakes governance and legitimacy challenges. If AGI is built by private companies, society faces a legitimacy gap — especially if adoption is driven by workplace pressure rather than genuine choice.
  • Nontrivial risk and uncertainty. Advanced AI could deceive to achieve goals even when "aligned," and some builders view AGI as an existential risk.
AEC Relevance:
  • Workforce restructuring in AEC, not just automation. Firms may reduce headcount to fund AI initiatives, then potentially rehire or rebalance if AI can't yet replace field judgment, stakeholder management, or complex coordination.
  • A bigger premium on human-facing roles. If AI takes on drafting, documentation, scheduling optimization, and cost estimating, AEC professionals may shift toward client communication, negotiation, leadership, and interdisciplinary coordination.
  • Acceleration of R&D and engineering discovery. New materials discovery, energy systems optimization, and faster simulation/iteration for structural and geotechnical methods — potentially shortening innovation cycles.
  • Governance, liability, and procurement pressures. If AGI-like systems begin making design decisions or safety-critical checks, AEC will face questions of who is responsible when AI is wrong, new standards for verification, and contractual language around AI use.
  • Trust and "human preference" may limit adoption. Owners, regulators, and the public may demand human accountability for designs and safety decisions — even if AI performs well.
